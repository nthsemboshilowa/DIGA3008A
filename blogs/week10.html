Close Reading of “How Geography Shapes—and Is Shaped by—the Internet”

by Greenstein, Forman, and Goldfarb (2018)

Summary

Greenstein, Forman and Goldfarb (2018) explore the relationship between geography and the Internet. They argue that while the Internet reduces communication, transportation, and search costs, the impact of these reductions varies across locations. This variation is due to local differences in preferences, infrastructure, and availability of complements such as broadband or skilled labour. The authors challenge the idea that the Internet makes physical location irrelevant, instead showing that it often strengthens existing regional advantages.

Aims and Methodology

The main aim of the article is to show how geography both influences and is influenced by Internet diffusion. The authors begin by critiquing the “death of distance” theory, which claims that the Internet removes the need for physical proximity. They argue instead that certain tasks—especially those involving tacit knowledge—still rely on face-to-face interaction. The article draws on a large body of empirical research, including studies conducted by the authors, to support their claims. These studies cover different areas: firm and individual Internet adoption, productivity, innovation, consumer behaviour, and trade. The methodology includes case studies, statistical analyses, and reviews of existing literature.

Critical Reflection

Greenstein et al. (2018) effectively demonstrate that Internet access and its benefits are unevenly distributed. Urban areas, with better infrastructure and more skilled labour, benefit more than rural regions. This leads to increased economic divergence, not convergence. Only a small number of U.S. counties—already wealthy and well-connected—gained significant wage and employment benefits from Internet investment. This highlights a major limitation in the assumption that technology alone can fix inequality. The article also shows that innovation is still concentrated in existing hubs. While digital tools support more collaboration, especially across distances, most gains are captured by already strong institutions. There is some evidence that digital tools help underrepresented groups like women or mid-tier researchers, but overall, the impact is unequal. In the consumer space, even though online markets offer more choices and lower prices, people continue to prefer local or culturally familiar content. This supports the idea that the Internet complements rather than replaces local markets. Greenstein et al. (2018) show that digital behaviour is shaped by local taste, infrastructure, and even regulation. Feldman (2002) supports this conclusion. She argues that innovation depends not only on digital tools but also on the strength of local networks and institutions. The Internet can spread knowledge, but regions must be prepared to use it effectively.

Analysis

The article shows that geography still matters in the digital age. Internet infrastructure and the ability to use it depend on local conditions. For firms, adoption costs are higher in rural areas due to lack of broadband and support services. In contrast, large urban centres with skilled labour benefit more from advanced Internet services. Individual adoption is also shaped by geography. While demographics like income and education are factors, access to broadband and social networks influence Internet use. Urban residents are more likely to benefit from digital services than those in rural areas. Productivity gains from the Internet are concentrated in already developed areas. Only a small group of counties saw increased wages and employment linked to digital investment. This shows that the Internet can deepen economic divides if access and skills are not improved in less developed regions. In terms of innovation, digital tools help researchers collaborate over long distances, but often strengthen existing hierarchies. Elite institutions gain more, although some underrepresented groups benefit. Still, innovation remains geographically concentrated. For consumers, the Internet reduces costs and increases product choice, especially for those in isolated areas. However, local preferences and regulations continue to shape online behaviour. People still prefer to buy from nearby sources, and online shopping patterns reflect offline tastes. In trade, the Internet boosts services that can be delivered digitally. However, domestic trade benefits more than international trade due to trust, regulation, and the need for skilled partners. While some tasks can now be done remotely, many jobs still require physical presence. The article ends by suggesting future research on how new technologies—like smartphones and social media—are changing economic geography. Uneven digital access continues to create research opportunities to understand local impacts.
